Optimization of the potential energy surfaces

It would be nice to be able to design – or suggest how to design – a molecular motor for specific properties (speed, force, torque, gearing, ability to work against a load, resistance to being forced backwards, or something else). To that end, we set out to explore the relationship between the shape of the potential energy surfaces and these properties.

Optimization of a single surface for maximal flux

To start, let’s begin with a fixed bound energy surface created by smoothing a sawtooth with seven spline points (Figure 2). I couldn’t find a way to spline across the periodic boundary, so the curve looks a little wonkier than expected. My first attempt was to use a downhill simplex method (Nelder-Mead optimization) to optimize the two surfaces together for flux. The results are not completely deterministic, even with with setting np.random.seed(42), and I don’t understand that.

After 1403 iterations, Nelder-Mead optimization results in the surface shown in Figure 3. Each blue line is an interation of the optimization. Lighter color correspond to earlier iterations. The final surface is darker because many lines are overlayed. I have not implemented bounds on the optimization because Nelder-Mead does not allow bounds, as far as I know. The result of this optimization is that the flux approaches −0.050 cycle s−1 quickly and stays there.

COBYLA and Powell’s method result in better optimization than simplex downhill.
